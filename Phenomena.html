Black Holes

Formed from collapsed massive stars, black holes warp space and time, consuming anything — even light — that crosses their event horizon. They are some of the most mysterious and powerful objects in the universe.

Supernovae

Supernovae are the explosive deaths of massive stars, releasing vast amounts of energy and spreading heavy elements across the cosmos — essential for forming planets, life, and new stars.

Auroras

These stunning light displays occur near Earth's poles when solar particles collide with atmospheric gases, producing glowing waves of green, purple, and red light in the sky.

Eclipses

When one celestial body moves into the shadow of another, eclipses occur — like solar eclipses when the Moon covers the Sun, or lunar eclipses when Earth's shadow falls on the Moon.

Nebulae

Nebulae are giant clouds of gas and dust in space, often forming beautiful, glowing structures. They're the birthplaces of stars and one of the most colorful features of the galaxy.

Pulsars

Pulsars are rapidly rotating neutron stars that emit beams of electromagnetic radiation. As they spin, these beams sweep across Earth like a cosmic lighthouse.

Gravitational Waves

Discovered in 2015, gravitational waves are ripples in space-time caused by massive objects like merging black holes or neutron stars. They confirm key predictions of Einstein’s relativity.

Gamma Ray Bursts

The brightest explosions in the universe, gamma-ray bursts release intense radiation for seconds or minutes. They’re thought to result from neutron star mergers or hypernovae.
